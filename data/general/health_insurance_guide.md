# Health Insurance Complete Guide

## What is Health Insurance?
Health insurance is a contract between you and the insurer where the insurer pays for your medical expenses in exchange for a regular premium. It protects you from high, unexpected medical costs.

## Types of Health Insurance Plans

### 1. Individual Health Insurance
- Covers a single person
- Separate sum insured for each member
- Best for: Single adults, people with pre-existing conditions

### 2. Family Floater Plan
- One sum insured shared across the entire family
- Usually cheaper than individual plans for each member
- Best for: Young healthy families
- Risk: Sum insured can get exhausted if multiple members claim

### 3. Senior Citizen Plans
- Designed for people aged 60+
- Higher premiums due to age
- Pre-existing diseases covered after shorter waiting period
- Examples: Star Senior Citizen Red Carpet, Niva Bupa Senior First

### 4. Critical Illness Plan
- Lump sum payout on diagnosis of listed critical illnesses
- Does NOT reimburse hospital bills - gives fixed amount
- Covered illnesses: Cancer, Heart Attack, Stroke, Kidney Failure, etc.
- Best used as a TOP-UP to regular health insurance

### 5. Top-Up and Super Top-Up Plans
- Kicks in after your base policy limit is exhausted
- Very affordable premiums
- Top-Up: Per claim basis
- Super Top-Up: Aggregate basis (better)

## Key Terms You Must Know

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Sum Insured (SI) | Maximum amount insurer pays in a year |
| Premium | Amount you pay to keep the policy active |
| Deductible | Amount you pay before insurance kicks in |
| Co-payment | % of claim you always pay (e.g. 10%) |
| Waiting Period | Period after policy start during which claims not allowed |
| Pre-Existing Disease (PED) | Any condition you had before buying policy |
| Cashless | Hospital directly bills insurer, you pay nothing |
| Reimbursement | You pay, then claim back from insurer |
| Network Hospital | Hospitals tied up with insurer for cashless |
| TPA | Third Party Administrator - manages claims |
| NCB | No Claim Bonus - SI increases if no claim made |

## Waiting Periods Explained

### Initial Waiting Period (30 days)
- No claims for first 30 days except accidents
- Every new policy has this

### Pre-Existing Disease Waiting Period (2-4 years)
- If you have diabetes, BP, thyroid etc., it's excluded for 2-4 years
- After this period, it IS covered
- This is why you should buy health insurance YOUNG before getting PEDs

### Specific Disease Waiting Period (1-2 years)
- Certain conditions like hernia, cataract, knee replacement
- Excluded for first 1-2 years even if not pre-existing

### Maternity Waiting Period (2-4 years)
- Maternity cover only kicks in after 2-4 years of continuous coverage

## How to Choose the Right Health Insurance

### Step 1: Calculate how much cover you need
- Minimum: Rs. 5 Lakhs per person
- Recommended: Rs. 10-15 Lakhs (medical inflation is 15%+ per year)
- Metro city residents: Rs. 20-25 Lakhs recommended

### Step 2: Individual vs Floater
- If all members are healthy and young → Family Floater
- If someone has chronic condition → Individual plans

### Step 3: Check network hospitals
- Verify your preferred hospitals are in the network
- Check hospital count in your city

### Step 4: Compare waiting periods
- Lower PED waiting period = better
- Check if your conditions are covered

### Step 5: Check claim settlement ratio
- Should be above 90%
- Star Health: 99.1%, Niva Bupa: 91.4%, HDFC ERGO: 89.2%

## Common Claim Rejection Reasons
1. Non-disclosure of pre-existing diseases at time of buying
2. Claiming during waiting period
3. Treatment not medically necessary
4. Excluded procedure or condition
5. Policy lapsed due to non-payment
6. Delay in intimating insurer

## Health Insurance Tax Benefits
- Premiums paid for self/family: Deduction up to Rs. 25,000 (Section 80D)
- Premiums for parents (below 60): Additional Rs. 25,000
- Premiums for senior citizen parents: Additional Rs. 50,000
- Maximum deduction possible: Rs. 1,00,000 per year
